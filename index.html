The Village of Hobbiton was filmed on an originally bare stretch of farmland in Matamata, New Zealand. In 1988, a team of location scouts working for Peter Jackson took an aerial view of New Zealand to search for a tall tree near a pond similar to the Party Tree described in the books. The rolling hills made for perfect hobbit holes and much greenery, and other set-pieces were installed and in the farm's long transformation, the crew built at least thirty distinct Hobbit-holes excluding Bag End.
Many forms of body doubles were used in "The Lord of the Rings" in order to visually show the size difference between various characters. In this scene, the team brought in an actor who is 4 ft 2 in. Then the editing team applied Frodo's actor's face onto the double actor to produce the short stature of Frodo.
Peter Jackson used real-life models referred to as "big-atures" which are large, hand-crafted versions of the enormous buildings seen in the film. This bigature was created with the combined efforts of 14 people and took 1200 hours. By using miniatures, controlling the lighting and the movement of the camera is possible. Shooting a miniature while having an enormous size allows there to be maximum detail to keep the scenes as realistic looking as manageable. Peter Jackson wanted to use as many bigatures as possible because it lets the filmmakers use real photography for the background and utilize digital characters to populate the scene.
To produce the size disparity, Gandalf and Bilbo are on different sets. Gandalf stands in front of a blue screen and Bilbo is on the set of his house. A body double (substitutes the actor) was used in place of Gandalf’s actor when the hat and wand were passed to Bilbo. Afterwards, the production team combined the two shots into one to portray the characters interacting.
Weta Workshop designed clothes for several different races such as Hobbits, Men, Dwarves and Elves but each race had different cultures such as the Men of Rohan and Gondor and the Elves of Rivendell and Cares Galadhon. In addition, the sheer number of costumes that were needed for the actors, stunt doubles, body doubles and extras numbered over 18,000 across the whole trilogy.
Aside from using CGI and child body doubles, the filmmaking team also employed brilliant forced perspective techniques that tricked viewers with optical illusions. The arts team created two different sized cups, tables, teapots etc. There were two versions of the same prop to broaden the illusion of the size dispartiy.
A software called Massive was developed and utilized during the making of The Lord of the Rings trilogy. Massive is an industry-first system using AI-driven autonomous agents (characters) to generate animation. Any large-scale scene that required hundreds of thousands of characters was created with Massive. Since then, it has developed into a complete product and has been licensed by a number of other visual effects houses. Massive has been in use for several films such as Harry Potter, The Chronicles of Narnia, Avatar and Avengers: Endgame.
Digital characters like the Cave Troll were created by modelling a sculpture of clay to represent the Cave Troll. Afterwards, the sculpture was scanned using a laser scanner to copy the model to be accessible virtually. When the chain from its neck is pulled back, its movements look vivid and natural. Even when using its hammer, the audience can sense the weight of the weapon.
Gollum is a combination of computer generation and live-action to create a digital actor. The idea of a fully CGI character was unfamiliar in the film industry at the time. Andy Serkis (Gollum’s actor) wore a motion capture suit and mimicked Gollum’s movements to capture the movements then applied it to Gollum’s animation.
